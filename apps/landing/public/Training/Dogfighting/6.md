# 6. Decoupled and the brake

**[Video: Basic drills — decoupled, take off & landing, tracking](https://youtu.be/BUsOaQDAjc8)**

**Focus:** Coupled vs decoupled and the space brake; when to use each; your vector stays until you change it or hit the brake.

**Why it matters:** Orbiting and pip control only make sense in decoupled. In coupled the ship fights your inputs; in decoupled you have raw physics and one button to stop.

In space there's no air friction. If you thrust in a direction, you keep going until you thrust the other way or hit the brake. **Coupled mode:** the ship automatically fires thrusters to cancel drift and try to match throttle. Nose and travel tend to align; it feels like a plane. **Decoupled mode:** the ship does *not* auto-cancel. You thrust once and you keep going that way until you counter it or hit the **space brake**. One brake input = full stop. Your **vector** (the direction you're moving through space) stays until you change it. Coupled = assisted; decoupled = raw physics.

Orbiting is easy in decoupled: nose on a target, add some strafe, and you glide in a circle without constant input. In coupled the ship would keep killing your drift so you'd have to tap constantly. In a fight, coupled works against you — hard to match another ship's speed, hard to hold a smooth orbit.

**When to use each** — Coupled is good for travel, landing, and when you want the ship to point where you go. Decoupled is good for precise aim, orbit strafing, and PvP. Know which you're in and switch when it helps. Fly decoupled for combat and practice; use coupled as a parking brake or for very smooth landings (TVI on the pad, ease down with brake).

**Checkpoint:** You understand the difference between coupled and decoupled, you've tried both, and you can fly a clean orbit in decoupled using the brake when you want to stop.
