# 12. Turn rate and SPViewer

**Focus:** Turn rate wins duels; roll then pitch is fastest; SPViewer shows your ship's numbers so you can fly to its strengths.

**Why it matters:** Two pilots in a rate fight are matching turn rates. If you don't know yours — or that your ship has strong up thrust — you're guessing.

*Rate* in a fight means **turn rate** — how fast you can put your nose on target. Ships have different pitch, yaw, and roll speeds (degrees per second). On almost every ship, **roll is fastest, then pitch, then yaw**. So the fastest way to get your nose on a target that's off to the side is: **roll** so they're above or below you, then **pitch** onto them. That's "roll then pitch" or "keeping the fight in pitch." If you yaw across, you're using the slowest axis. The **rate fight** is when both pilots are doing this — matching turn rates, orbiting, trying to keep the pip in pitch. Whoever's ship has better turn rate (or whoever gets on target first) has the advantage.

**Ship performance and SPViewer**  
Your ship's **speed wall** (max SCM speed without boost), **pitch / yaw / roll** rates, and **strafe strength** (G's in each direction) define how it flies. **SPViewer** (spviewer.eu) is a community tool that lists these numbers for every ship. Use it to: look up your ship's speed wall so you know what to stay under; compare pitch/yaw/roll so you know why "roll then pitch" matters; and compare ships before you fly or buy. Key blocks: Flight Performances (SCM speed, boost, pitch/yaw/roll), Accelerations (main, retro, strafe up/down/left/right in G's). Many ships have strong up thrust — so **put the enemy above you, then pitch up and strafe up**; that uses your fastest rotation (pitch) and often your strongest strafe. No need to memorise — just know where to look when you want to understand why your ship feels different from another.

**Checkpoint:** You know where to find your ship's turn rates and speed wall (SPViewer), and you can say why "enemy above, pitch and strafe up" is good advice for most ships.
